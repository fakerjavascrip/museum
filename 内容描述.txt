 Exhibit Labels

1.Artifact: 3d printed gun
Character: Daniel, Tonny and Octavio
In chapter Daniel Gonzales “the gun only took three hours to print.” (Orange 187). This represents the democratization of violence through modern digital tools. Unlike traditional weapons it only took hours to have, lack serial numbers and can easily evade metal detectors. This reflects the grim reality that the systemic trauma experienced by the city’s machine often remains in stealth hidden mode, for marginalized characters like Tonny and Octavio, this untraceable weapon rapidly transforms into a destructive prosthesis of false empowerment. This artifact critically highlights the tragic consequences when advanced digital tools are diverted toward immediate violence rather than long term cultural improvement or collective self-reconstruction. It deeply proves that while technology grants a temporary sense of presence in the painful absence of ancestral territory, without proper cultural grounding, it only causes historical, generational wounds to reopen in more hidden, decentralized, and lethal ways. 



2.Artifact: TV
Character: Orvil Red Feather
In chapter Orvil Red Feather, “Orvil knew he wanted to dance the first time he saw a dancer on TV.” (Orange 121). For Orvil, seeing a Native dancer on TV was the exact moment that made him realized he genuinely wanted to connect with his culture and heritage. Since he never had traditional elders or family to teach him, to let him know, the TV became his only way to learn who he was. Orvil rightfully should have learned this dance from his grandmother, but instead he had to find it on a screen. The TV symbolizes both hope and profound loss, it gave Orvil something with fragments to hold onto, but it also heavily reminds us how much was taken from him. Technology becomes a desperate tool for Native characters to rebuild their identity when everything else has been stripped away, forcing them to piece together an identity from whatever images remain.



3.Artifact: the fallen drone
Character: Bill Davis, Daniel 
In the chapter Bill Davis, “Bill sees that it’s headed for home plate. he skips down the stairs. he hasn’t gotten moving this fast in years.” (Orange 87). it is a piece of modern technology that was sent by the robbers to spy on the powwow before the attack. In this moment, bill, an old man, suddenly moves faster than he has in years because he sees the drone coming. The drone does not just represent a robbery plan, it forces everyone at the powwow to react, including people like bill who just came to enjoy the event. It shows how technology can invade a space that is supposed to be safe and sacred for native people. The powwow is one of the only places where characters like Orvil and others can feel connected to their culture, and the drone turns it into a target. This is the dark side of the exhibit’s theme, technology is not always used for healing. Sometimes it is used to take, to surveil, and to destroy the very spaces where native identity is being rebuilt.








4.Artifact：Dene’s Digital Camera
Character:  Dene and his uncle
As the novel reveals, the documentary project to record the stories of urban native Americans “How it was originally his uncle’s idea” (Orange 33). This artifact, a digital video camera, belongs to Dene, but its origins are deeply rooted in family legacy and loss. By taking up this camera, dene is not just using a piece of technology. He is actively keeping his uncle’s spirit alive and piecing together a fractured community. Unlike historical media that defined native identity through racist or external lenses, this digital tool grants narrative sovereignty directly to people. The camera acts as a digital loom, weaving together the scattered, invisible voices of urban Indians into a collective memory. It proves when physical land and traditional elders are lost, modern technology can serve as the new medium for the oral tradition, allowing a displaced community to actively reconstruct and protect their shared identity.




5.Artifact: The rolling Indian Head
Character:  all characters
 In the Prologue, Orange references a Native story where "While they ate, a head rolled in. They ran, and the head followed them.” (Orange 6). Orange uses this image to talk about how the Indian head test pattern, a picture of native man in headdress, was placed on tv screens before any programs started. Just like the head in the story that keeps following people no matter where they run, the Indian head image could not be escaped. It was everywhere, every day, on every program. But this image was not made by native people, it was made for non-native audiences who wanted a symbol, not a real person. Native people were turned into a logo. Image has always been used in a way that feels inescapable and threatening, not welcoming. It shows the dark history of how media and technology were used to define native identity without native people’s voices. Every character in the novel is trying to fight back against this kind of erasure and take control of their own story.


6.Artifact: Daniel’s VR Goggles
Character: Daniel
This artifact is worn by Daniel as he pilots his drone over the big Oakland powwow. Sitting isolated in his basement, “Daniel has his VR Goggles on.” (Orange 265) ，using technology to experience the event from a safe, detached distance. This object complicates the exhibit’s central thesis by highlighting the isolating and disembodying effects of modern digital tools. While technology throughout the novel often serves to piece identity back together, Daniel’s use of the VR headset represents a tragic detachment. Instead of engaging with his culture in the physical world, the sacred gathering is reduced to a simulated video feed on a screen. The goggles symbolize a hypermodern form of alienation, proving that a virtual, bird’s-eye view of a territory cannot substitute for the human connection of standing on the land with one’s people. Ultimately, this artifact turns Daniel into a passive, helpless spectator to the violence that unfolds, showing how technology can paralyze rather than heal.
